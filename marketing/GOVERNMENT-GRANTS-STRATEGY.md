## PART 6: SMALL BUSINESS ADMINISTRATION (SBA) GRANTS

The **SBA does NOT give direct grants** in the traditional sense, but offers several grant-like programs:

### 6.1 SBA SBIR/STTR Programs (Quasi-Grants)

**Key Advantage:** Non-dilutive funding with no equity requirement

**Program Types:**

#### A) Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR)
- **Phases:** Phase 1 ($50K-$150K), Phase 2 ($350K-$500K), Phase 3 (non-TA)
- **For:** For-profit companies <500 employees
- **Timeline:** 6-month Phase 1, 24-month Phase 2
- **Industries:** Tech-enabled healthcare, AI, biotech
- **Agencies:** 11 federal agencies participate (NIH, NSF, DOD, DOE, etc.)

**FOR THE KIDS Eligibility:**
- If structured as for-profit (requires nonprofit status analysis)
- Subsidiary structure: For-profit operates platform, nonprofit receives 60% revenue

**Application Steps:**
1. Register on sbir.gov
2. Search active solicitations (deadlines vary by agency)
3. Prepare Phase 1 proposal (25-30 pages max)
4. Submit through agency portal

**Phase 1 Proposal Sections:**
- Executive summary (2 pages)
- Scientific/technical merit (10 pages)
- Market potential (5 pages)
- Budget narrative (3-5 pages)
- Management approach (3-5 pages)

**Phase 1 Success Rate:** ~15-20%, Phase 2 success rate: ~40-60% (of Phase 1 winners)

#### B) Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR)
- **Similar to SBIR** but requires academic/nonprofit research partner
- **Phases:** Phase 1 ($50K-$100K), Phase 2 ($300K-$400K)
- **Requirements:** Partnership with university or nonprofit research institution
- **Advantage:** Academic credibility helps FOR THE KIDS' research positioning

**Best Agencies for FOR THE KIDS SBIR/STTR:**
1. **NIH (NICHD)** - Pediatric health research
2. **NSF** - AI/ML and health tech innovation
3. **DoD** (if applicable to health resilience)
4. **ARPA-H** - High-risk health innovation
5. **DoE** - If incorporating energy/efficiency tech

### 6.2 SBA 7(a) Loan Program (Not Grants, but Worth Mentioning)

## PART 9: GRANT WRITING BEST PRACTICES

### 9.1 Grant Writing Fundamentals

**The Golden Formula:**
```
IMPACT = (PROBLEM SEVERITY) � (SOLUTION EFFECTIVENESS) � (TEAM CAPABILITY)

Grant Score = Impact + Feasibility + Budget Efficiency
```

### 9.2 Essential Grant Writing Components

#### A) Problem Statement (The "Why")

**What Makes a Strong Problem Statement:**
- Quantified: "2.3 million uninsured children lack access to preventive care" (not vague: "kids need healthcare")
- Localized: Show problem in specific geography/community
- Personal: Include 1-2 specific case examples/stories
- Data-backed: Cite authoritative sources (CDC, WHO, academic journals)
- Connected to FOR THE KIDS mission: Link to how 60% charity model addresses it

**Example Problem Statement:**
```
"In the United States, approximately 7.4 million children (9.3% of
child population) lack health insurance, with disproportionate impact
on Hispanic (12.8%), African American (10.1%), and rural children.
Current charity models lack transparency, causing supporter distrust and
inefficient fund allocation. FOR THE KIDS addresses this through
AI-enabled health equity assessment and blockchain-verified 60% revenue
distribution to verified pediatric charities, creating both health
access and philanthropic transparency."
```

#### B) Theory of Change (The "How")

**Structure:**
```
IF [we do X action]
THEN [Y change will occur]
BECAUSE [Z logical reason]
LEADING TO [ultimate impact]
```

**FOR THE KIDS Example:**
```
IF we build an AI-powered platform connecting pediatric charities with
transparent fund allocation verification,

THEN healthcare access inequity in underserved communities will decrease,

BECAUSE:
1) Charities get real-time impact data enabling better resource allocation
2) supporters see verified 60% allocation increasing trust and contributions
3) AI identifies gaps in pediatric care coverage, directing funds to
   highest-need areas

LEADING TO:
- 60% increase in verified charity capacity by 2027
- 30% improvement in pediatric health metrics in target communities
- $X million additional charitable funding raised through transparency
```

#### C) Goals & Objectives (SMART Criteria)

**SMART = Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound**

**Weak Objective:** "Improve health outcomes for kids"

### 9.5 Common Grant Writing Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Matters | Fix |
|---------|----------------|-----|
| **Vague problem statement** | Funder can't see why they should care | Quantify: "2M kids lack access" not "kids have needs" |
| **Unclear connection between problem/solution** | Seems pie-in-the-sky | Explain causal chain: Problem?FOR THE KIDS?Specific impact |
| **Overpromising outcomes** | Loss of credibility if you miss targets | Be conservative, beat targets, build reputation |
| **Weak evaluation plan** | Can't prove impact ? no future funding | Include independent evaluator, specific metrics |
| **Requesting too much time from staff** | Unfeasible implementation = rejection | Budget realistically for charity partner engagement |
| **Unclear budget** | Raises fraud/waste concerns | Itemize every expense, show value per dollar |
| **Generic proposal** | Doesn't show specific fit for program | Mention program officer by name, cite foundation priorities |
| **Missing letters of support** | Unproven community buy-in | Get 5-10 strong letters from implementation partners |
| **Poor writing/typos** | Shows lack of professionalism | Hire editor, have 3+ people review before submission |
| **Wrong deadline/program fit** | Instant rejection | Read requirements 3x, call program officer to confirm |

## PART 3: DISCUSSION STARTER COMMENTS

### Comment Strategy by Topic

These are 10 existing discussion types where you should insert thoughtful comments (don't self-promote unless genuinely relevant).

#### Comment 1: "How to Fund Your Startup"

**Context:** Discussion about funding models, investor dynamics

**Your angle:** Bootstrapped approach + structural allocation

```
We took a different approach: we eliminated the cap table conflict.

60% of our revenue goes directly to verified charities (automated via smart
contract on Base). No founder later says "we should keep more." No investor
asks "why are we giving this away?" The structure makes the decision for us.

Year 1 result: simpler operations, clearer decision-making, customers who
specifically chose us because of it.

The trap most founders hit: doing good as a *feature* instead of
*infrastructure*. If it's a feature, it dies when margin pressure hits.
If it's architecture, it survives because the money goes out automatically.

Curious if anyone else has tried embedding a business decision this deep
into the company structure.

FOR THE KIDS - https://aidoesitall.website
```

#### Comment 2: "Business Model Questions"

**Context:** Discussion about recurring revenue, pricing, profitability

**Your angle:** Transparency forces honest accounting

```
Unpopular opinion: if your business model isn't transparent enough to
put on a blockchain, it probably isn't as clean as you think.

We publish all our allocations on-chain. Every dollar in, we can show exactly
where 60 cents goes.

This forced us to:
1. Actually define "profit" (accounting gets real fast)
2. Know our real unit economics (no fudging numbers)
3. Build to scale from day one (fees/overhead matter when transparent)

Side effect: investors actually respect it more because numbers can't lie.

The hard part isn't the ethics. It's the accounting discipline. But that's
kind of a good hard part to have.

Anyone else using blockchain for financial transparency (not as a product,
but as internal infrastructure)?
```

#### Comment 3: "Building in Public"

**Context:** Discussion about transparency, sharing progress

**Your angle:** Business model + transparency = accelerated trust

```
Building in public + transparent business model = trust acceleration.

We share revenue allocations alongside product updates. Some think that's
oversharing (it's not - it's the business model).

What we found: people are way more likely to use your product when they
know exactly where their money goes. It's not about being "nice" - it's
about reducing uncertainty.

Revenue allocation as marketing is cheap. Revenue allocation as
*architecture* is how you win.

Curious: does anyone else use their business model as a founding competitive advantage?
```

#### Comment 4: "DAO Governance"

**Context:** Discussion about decentralized decision-making, token models

**Your angle:** DAOs work better when they solve one specific problem

```
Most DAOs fail because they try to decentralize *everything*.

We used DAOs for one specific decision: which charities receive our 60%
allocation. That's it. One decision, democratized, on-chain, immutable.

Why this works:
- It's a decision with clear scope (not fuzzy)
- The outcome is auditable (money goes where votes said)
- Bad actors have no advantage (you can't profit from voting)

The rest runs normally (founder makes operational decisions, engineering
ships features).

This combo of "centralized operations + decentralized allocation" seems
way more sustainable than full DAO governance.

Any governance people here experimenting with hybrid models?
```

#### Comment 5: "AI Infrastructure Costs"

**Context:** Discussion about scaling AI, compute expenses, margin compression

**Your angle:** Constraints force efficiency, not compromise

```
AI is so expensive that margin collapse is real. We had to make a choice:
either cut our allocation % or build smarter infrastructure.

We built smarter. Used DAO governance to let users vote on our cloud provider
(they picked the one with best carbon footprint, not just cheapest).

Result: margins improved while allocation stayed constant.

The point: don't let "doing good" be separate from "building efficiently."
They should inform each other from day one.

Props to any founders using constraint (like a fixed allocation %) as
innovation pressure.
```

#### Comment 6: "Transparency vs. Privacy"

**Context:** Discussion about sharing business metrics, privacy concerns

**Your angle:** Different problems require different solutions

```
We put our entire financial model on Base where anyone can audit it.

People ask: "Isn't that a privacy risk?"

Honestly? No. Our privacy risk isn't financial transparency - it's data
security. Those are different problems. You can have both.

Financial transparency actually helps us:
- Customers trust we're not lying about allocations
- Charity partners trust money arrives as promised
- Employees see numbers match the mission

The tricky part: being transparent about money without being reckless
about other data (customer PII, contract details, etc.).

Any builders in here wrestling with this? What's your balance?
```

#### Comment 7: "Founder Salary and Ethics"

**Context:** Discussion about founder compensation, values-aligned companies

**Your angle:** Visible founder salary changes behavior

```
This is probably controversial, but: founder pay should be visible.

We publish ours (10% of revenue - about half market rate for our role).
Not to virtue signal. Because it changes behavior in a useful way:

1. Every expense matters more (you feel the tradeoff viscerally)
2. You can't hide bad decisions in overhead
3. Investors respect founders who've thought about their own incentives

The weird outcome: we get better team members than when I was trying to
maximize personal comp. People want to work somewhere the numbers make sense.

Might be industry-specific, but curious if anyone else has experimented
with radically transparent founder compensation?
```

#### Comment 8: "Measuring Impact"

**Context:** Discussion about metrics, KPIs, measuring what matters

**Your angle:** Impact is output + outcome

```
The hardest metric: did we actually do good?

We track the obvious stuff ($ to charities), but that's easy. The hard question:
did those dollars move the needle on real outcomes?

Our approach: ask the charities directly. Not in a compliance way - actually
talk to the researchers about whether our allocation moved their work forward.

This led to a shift: instead of giving 60% to whoever, we now vote on charities
where *our specific users* can have measurable impact. An API for one charity
vs. another has different ROI.

It's weird to apply "ROI" to charity. But if you're committed to allocation,
you owe it to yourself to make those dollars count.

How do other builders measure impact beyond just "did we do the thing?"
```

#### Comment 9: "Startup Fatigue and Purpose"

**Context:** Discussion about burnout, founder mental health, staying motivated

**Your angle:** Structure solves for purpose

```
Real talk: the reason I haven't burned out in Year 1 is because the business
model aligns with my actual values.

When revenue goes up, something I care about gets more funding. I don't have
to rationalize it. I don't have to feel guilty about profit. I don't have to
argue with investors about "social responsibility."

The structure decided it. Now I just optimize for operational excellence.

This is probably only available to founders who can afford to take 10% instead
of 30%. So I'm aware of the privilege here. But if you *can* structure your
company this way, I think it's worth considering, just for mental health ROI.

Anyone else find that purpose-aligned business model actually improves execution?
```

#### Comment 10: "Building with AI Ethics"

**Context:** Discussion about AI safety, responsibility, vendor selection

**Your angle:** Abstract ethics becomes concrete infrastructure

```
Most "ethical AI" conversations are abstract. We made it concrete: our profit
allocation funds research at children's hospitals studying AI's effects.

So when we make infrastructure decisions, we know where the margin goes. We can
actually ask: "does this decision help or hurt the research we fund?"

Examples:
- Chose cloud provider with best AI safety practices (even if slightly pricier)
- Open-sourced our allocation contracts (competitors can copy the model)
- Contributed compute time to pediatric research initiatives

The ROI: it makes decision-making *faster*, not slower. You have fewer options
when you filter for "does this align" + "is it quality" + "can we afford it?"

Constraints are features.

How are other builders incorporating their values into infrastructure decisions?
```

## PART 6: COMMUNITY MANAGEMENT GUIDELINES

### Responding to Different Comment Types

#### Type 1: Technical Questions (Tier 1 - under 2 hours)

**Example:** "How is the smart contract secured against price oracle attacks?"

**Response framework:**
1. Acknowledge the good question
2. Give specific technical answer
3. Link to audit/code
4. Invite deeper review

```
Great question. We use a time-weighted average price feed from Chainlink
(redundant nodes, tamper-resistant). For charity allocations, we're not price-sensitive
(just moving stablecoins), so oracle manipulation isn't a vector.

That said, we had this audited by [firm]. You can review: [Basescan link]

If you spot an issue we missed, I'd rather know now. Bounty available.
```

#### Type 2: Skepticism/Pushback (Tier 1 - take seriously)

**Example:** "This seems like a marketing gimmick. How do I know you won't change the model later?"

**Response framework:**
1. Don't get defensive
2. Acknowledge the legitimate concern
3. Provide proof/mechanism
4. Invite verification

```
Fair criticism. I'd be skeptical too.

The difference: it's not a promise, it's code. Here's how we made it immutable:

1. Smart contract on Base (auditable forever)
2. Legal docs require new owner (if acquired) to maintain allocation
3. beneficiary allocation addresses are hardcoded (can't be changed)
4. DAO voting prevents me from unilaterally changing split

You could audit all of this. Not as a faith exercise - as a technical review.

What would make you trust this more?
```

#### Type 3: Product Questions (Tier 2 - under 6 hours)

**Example:** "Does your API support streaming responses?"

**Response framework:**
1. Give straightforward answer
2. Provide link to docs
3. Offer setup help
4. Invite feedback on feature gaps

```
Yes, we support both streaming and batch APIs. Streaming is lower-latency,
batch is cheaper (better unit economics = more to allocate).

Docs: [link]

If you're building something where this matters, happy to help with setup.
What's your use case?
```

#### Type 4: Partnership/Collaboration (Tier 1 - under 2 hours)

**Example:** "We run a charity focused on pediatric research. Interested in exploring this together?"

**Response framework:**
1. Express genuine interest
2. Take conversation offline (not appropriate for comment thread)
3. Provide direct contact
4. Set clear expectations

```
Absolutely interested. This is exactly the kind of partnership we're building toward.

Not the right place for a detailed conversation, but let's continue this offline.

[Your email or contact method]

Looking forward to exploring this.
```

### Handling Criticism Constructively

**When someone attacks the model:**
```
This is helpful pushback. We're still figuring this out, and criticism is
how we improve.

Specific question: [restate their concern as a real problem]

Here's how we're thinking about it: [explain mechanism]

Where do you see the gap? [invite specific feedback]
```

**When someone thinks it's too good to be true:**
```
I get it. The gap between "profit allocation in code" and "actually doing it"
is where most companies fail.

Here's what makes us different:
- [Structural mechanism that prevents backsliding]
- [Personal stake/financial incentive to keep commitment]
- [Governance that prevents founder override]

Still skeptical? [Invite them to audit / verify / challenge]
```

## PART 8: VOICE & TONE GUIDELINES

### Do's
- Lead with technical substance
- Be specific (contract addresses, real numbers, audit links)
- Admit uncertainty ("We don't know yet")
- Invite verification ("Please audit this")
- Focus on builder appeal (respect integrity > emotion)
- Update regularly (stale info kills credibility)
- Show your work (link to code, docs, contracts)

### Don'ts
- Sound preachy about helping children
- Position as non-profit (it's for-profit with structured allocation)
- Hide the business model
- Use emotional charity language
- Oversell the ethical angle
- Use hype words (revolutionary, world-changing, etc.)
- Make promises you can't guarantee

### Tone
- **Transparent** about constraints and unknowns
- **Specific** about numbers and mechanisms
- **Humble** about what you don't know
- **Confident** about what's structural vs aspirational
- **Curious** about other builders' approaches
- **Non-defensive** when challenged
- **Action-oriented** (focus on execution, not intentions)
